# Power Budgeting and Rail Isolation

This document covers power budgeting and rail isolation for PhoenixRooivalk
prototype and integration hardware to ensure reliable operation and to avoid
noise or fault propagation between subsystems.

## Objectives

- Allocate power budget per subsystem (sensors, compute, actuation, comms) with
  margin for peak loads.
- Define rail topology: shared vs isolated rails, and where isolation is
  required for safety or EMC.
- Document fusing, reverse-polarity protection, and sequencing where applicable.

## Subsystem Budgets

| Subsystem | Typical role          | Notes                                         |
| --------- | --------------------- | --------------------------------------------- |
| Detection | Sensors, RF front-end | Sensitive to noise; consider isolated rail    |
| Compute   | SoC/MCU, peripherals  | Peak current during boot/flash                |
| Actuation | Servos, drivers       | High transient currents; isolate from sensing |
| Comms     | Wi-Fi, serial, USB    | Shared or dedicated rail per design           |

Values and rails should be filled in per platform (e.g. RKV-M, turret demo
unit).

## Rail Isolation

- **Why** — Prevent motor/servo transients from affecting sensors and logic;
  meet safety separation where control and actuation are on different rails.
- **Where** — Document which rails are isolated (e.g. DC-DC with isolation,
  separate batteries) and which share a common ground or return.
- **Grounding** — Single-point vs star ground; keep high-current returns away
  from signal paths.

## Safety and Fusing

- Overcurrent protection per rail or per load as appropriate.
- Document behavior on undervoltage (brown-out) so that control logic and safety
  controller can enforce safe state (e.g. disable actuation, hold position).
